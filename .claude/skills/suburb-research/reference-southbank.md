## Executive summary (`thesis_main`)

Southbank's apartment median sits at $565,000 as at February 2026, down 4.8% over five years and 1.7% over three (Source: CoreLogic, Feb 2026), placing it in the bottom quartile of capital-city sub-markets on 10-year price growth of 1.9% per year compounded. New-build pricing of $11,300 to $13,500 per square metre of apartment floor area makes Southbank the cheapest functional inner-CBD apartment market in the country (Source: Charter Keck Cramer, Feb 2026). The closest comparable cycle bottoms are Sydney CBD in 2012 and Brisbane CBD in 2018, both of which posted multi-year price falls inside tight vacancy and an undersupplied state, then ran 5 to 8 year recoveries averaging 5% to 7% per year (Source: CoreLogic Quarterly Capital City Series, Jan 2026).

Every forward-looking signal in the suburb points the opposite way to the recent five-year price drop. Vacancy printed 1.3% in March 2026, down from 6.0% at the September 2021 peak and below the 2.0% threshold that defines a tight market (Source: SQM Research, Mar 2026), with the tightening continuous for 11 consecutive months. Median weekly rent has lifted 9.4% on the 1-bed and 7.1% on the 2-bed over twelve months, and has risen in 22 of the past 24 months. Gross rental yield of 5.5% to 6.0% sits materially ahead of Sydney CBD at 4.2% and Brisbane CBD at 4.9%. Rental income carries the investment while prices catch up.

Beneath the headline price the underlying balance has shifted hard. The City of Melbourne 2024 to 2041 forecast adds about 22,000 residents, requiring roughly 12,900 new dwellings or about 800 per year at the suburb's 1.7-person average household (Source: City of Melbourne forecast.id, Mar 2025; ABS Census 2021). Against that 17-year demand horizon the visible for-sale apartment pipeline (those under construction or approved by council) totals 1,383 units across five named projects and absorbs in 1.7 years, after which currently approved supply runs dry until the next 4 to 5 year development cycle responds. Statewide, Victoria has approved approximately 60,000 dwellings annually against the Housing Statement target of 80,000, leaving a government-acknowledged structural shortfall of 20,000 to 23,000 per year (Source: Victorian Government Housing Statement, Sep 2025). Southbank is land-locked between the Yarra, the rail corridor, the Domain Parklands and CityLink, with about 85% of its developable footprint already built or approved for build.

The catalyst layer is in hand. Metro Tunnel opened in December 2025, with Anzac Station 200 metres east of Southbank Boulevard cutting travel to Melbourne Central to 8 minutes (Source: Rail Projects Victoria, Nov 2025). Fishermans Bend renewal is committed at $9.4 billion over 10 years and adds 80,000 jobs immediately south (Source: Department of Transport and Planning Victoria, Jan 2026). Council-area price-to-income of 6.4x compares with Sydney 9.1x and Brisbane 7.3x (Source: ANZ CoreLogic Housing Affordability Report, Q1 2026), keeping the suburb inside the affordability band that supports the 22% first-home-buyer share already running in the past twelve months. The report sits at a conviction score of 80, Strong Buy.

## CORE pillars (12)

### 1. Demographics — `demographics`

**Headline:** Population +18.4% in 5 years to 32,400, dominated by 25-44 year old renters.

**Stats:** Population (2024) 32,400 · 5yr growth +18.4% · Median age 30 · Renter share 71%

Population sits at 32,400 as at 30 June 2024 (Source: ABS official population estimate, cat. 3218.0, Mar 2025), up from 27,400 in 2019. Median age 30, with couples without children plus lone-person households accounting for 68% of dwellings (Source: ABS Census 2021), the highest of any inner-Melbourne suburb. Household income median $98,400 (Source: ATO postcode 3006 statistics, FY 2023). Tenure mix is 71% renter, 29% owner-occupier (Source: ABS Census 2021).

### 2. Migration — `migration`

**Headline:** Net overseas migration into the council area running +29,800 in 2023-24, a structural Southbank tailwind.

**Stats:** Net overseas (council area) +29,800 · Net interstate (council area) -1,400

City of Melbourne council area captured net overseas migration of +29,800 in 2023-24 (Source: ABS cat. 3101.0, Sep 2024), of which Southbank attracts a disproportionate share given its student- and skilled-migrant-rental orientation. Net interstate is mildly negative (-1,400 council-area-wide), a reversal that began in late 2023 as Melbourne pricing reset relative to Sydney and Brisbane.

### 3. Employment — `employment`

**Headline:** Tenant pool anchored by Southbank Arts Precinct, Crown, and the southern CBD office spine.

**Stats:** Suburb jobs ~14,000 · 1km jobs ~85,000 · Council area unemployment 4.1% · Wage growth (12 months) 4.3%

The Southbank Arts Precinct (Arts Centre, NGV International, Melbourne Recital Centre) and Crown Entertainment Complex employ about 14,000 directly within the suburb (Source: Creative Victoria, Feb 2025; Crown Resorts annual report, Sep 2025). The southern CBD office spine within 1km adds about 85,000 jobs (Source: City of Melbourne employment survey, Sep 2024). Unemployment in the council area is 4.1% (Source: ABS Labour Force, Feb 2026), in line with the Greater Melbourne average. Wage growth 4.3% over twelve months (Source: ABS cat. 6345.0, Dec 2025).

### 4. Supply pipeline — `supply_pipeline`

**Headline:** 1,383 for-sale apartments underway or approved, the market soaks up in 1.7 years against ~800/yr demand, then pipeline runs dry.

**Stats:** Pipeline (for-sale) 1,383 units · Demand to 2041 ~12,900 dwellings · Demand p.a. ~800 · Absorption 1.7 years · Statewide shortfall 20-23k/yr

DEMAND. Southbank's resident forecast adds about 22,000 people between 2024 and 2041 (Source: City of Melbourne forecast.id, Mar 2025). At an average household size of 1.7 (Source: ABS Census 2021), that is about 12,900 new dwellings required, or about 800 per year over the 17-year horizon. SUPPLY. Named for-sale apartment pipeline currently underway or approved within Southbank totals 1,383 units across five projects: Moray House (305 units, completion 2026; Source: Salvo Group, Mar 2026), LAS Group Clarendon Street tower (510 units, completion 2027; Source: Urban Developer, Jan 2026), Indi Southbank Stage II (240 units, completion 2027; Source: Investa Indi, Feb 2026), Sapphire by the Gardens stage 2 (248 units, completion 2028; Source: Beulah, Dec 2025), plus minor smaller infill projects (about 80 units; Source: City of Melbourne planning register, Mar 2026). Indi Southbank Stage I (434 units) is build-to-rent (one landlord owns the whole building, leases out only) and already opened, so it absorbs tenant demand without competing for owner-occupier or investor purchase. ABSORPTION. Against ~800/yr demand the 1,383-unit for-sale pipeline takes 1.7 years for the market to soak up, after which on currently approved sites supply runs dry until the next development cycle responds (4 to 5 years from putting the build back out to bid through to completion). STATEWIDE. Victoria has approved approximately 60,000 dwellings annually against the Housing Statement target of 80,000 (Source: Victorian Government Housing Statement, Sep 2025; ABS cat. 8731, Feb 2026). Structural shortfall of 20,000 to 23,000 dwellings per year is a state-government-acknowledged figure. LAND. Bounded by the Yarra north, rail corridor and Domain Parklands east, West Gate Freeway/CityLink south, Maribyrnong/Yarra junction west. No remaining undeveloped land. Capital City Zone 1 (Melbourne's CBD planning zone) limits further density on already-maxed sites. About 85% of the developable footprint is built or approved by council. CONCLUSION. A 1.7-year clearing window into a 17-year demand horizon, in a state with a government-acknowledged 20,000+ annual shortfall, on land that physically cannot expand.

**Stats:** Vacancy (Mar 2026) 1.3% · Sep 2021 peak 6.0% · Months tightening 11

Vacancy printed 1.3% in March 2026 (Source: SQM Research, Mar 2026), down from 6.0% at the September 2021 peak. Below the 2.0% threshold that defines a tight market and well below the 3.0% oversupply line. The tightening has been continuous for 11 consecutive months.

### 6. Price growth — `price_growth`

**Headline:** Five-year median down 4.8%, a cycle-bottom signal not a reason to walk away.

**Stats:** Median apartment $565,000 · 5yr growth -4.8% · 10yr per year 1.9% · New-build $ per sqm $11.3k-$13.5k

Median apartment price $565,000 as at February 2026, down 4.8% over five years and 1.7% over three (Source: CoreLogic, Feb 2026). 10-year price growth 1.9% per year compounded, the bottom quartile of capital-city sub-markets. The recent price drop contradicts every forward-looking signal (vacancy, rent growth, supply absorption), which is the opportunity. Comparable cycle-bottom moments in Sydney CBD (2012) and Brisbane CBD (2018) were followed by 5 to 8 year recoveries averaging 5% to 7% per year. New-build pricing sits at $11,300 to $13,500 per square metre of apartment floor area, the cheapest functional inner-CBD apartment market in the country (Source: Charter Keck Cramer, Feb 2026).

### 7. Rent trend — `rent_trend`

**Headline:** 1-bed rents up 9.4% over twelve months, gross rental yield 5.7%, the strongest CBD yield in Australia.

**Stats:** 1-bed rent $620/wk · 2-bed rent $785/wk · 1-bed (12 months) +9.4% · Gross rental yield 5.5-6.0%

Gross rental yield 5.5% to 6.0% across the unit mix, materially ahead of Sydney CBD (4.2%) and Brisbane CBD (4.9%) (Source: SQM Research, Mar 2026). ### 8. Affordability — `affordability`

**Headline:** Price-to-income 6.4x, lowest of any capital-city CBD; eligible for self-managed super fund purchase.

**Stats:** Price/income 6.4x · First-home buyer share 22% · Mortgage repayments 38% of income

Council-area price-to-income 6.4x (Source: ANZ CoreLogic Housing Affordability Report, Q1 2026), versus Sydney 9.1x and Brisbane 7.3x. Lower is more affordable. ### 10. Risk register — `risk_register`

**Headline:** Six quantified risks, the largest being timing of apartment-cycle recovery and 1.7-year supply concentration.

**Stats:** Risks tracked 6 · Highest severity 4 (strata, flood) · Highest likelihood 4 (recovery lag, supply)

- **Risk 1.** Apartment recovery lags house cycle (severity 3, likelihood 4). Mitigation: long ownership period; rental income carries the investment while prices catch up.
- **Risk 2.** Concentrated 1.7-year supply cluster of 1,383 units into 2026-28 (severity 3, likelihood 4). Mitigation: stagger entry, target post-2028 settlements; short-term lease pressure manageable given 1.3% vacancy headroom.
- **Risk 3.** Build-cost reset re-opens supply taps (severity 3, likelihood 2). Mitigation: 4-5 year lag between project economics improving and completion gives the cycle room to run.
- **Risk 4.** Owners corporation governance and strata costs (severity 4, likelihood 3). Mitigation: target towers with established building maintenance funds (sinking funds) and recent pre-purchase strata reports.
- **Risk 5.** Yarra flood-prone overlay on about 12% of parcels (severity 4, likelihood 2). Mitigation: filter buy list to non-overlay parcels; insurer pre-clearance.
- **Risk 6.** Net interstate migration council-area-wide turns more negative (severity 2, likelihood 3). Mitigation: demand carried by overseas migration, structurally larger.

## Calibration notes for future runs

1. **Citation density.** Pillar narratives carry a `(Source: ...)` tag for every figure. Match this. A paragraph with an unsourced number is a fail.
2. **Dimension reasoning.** Each dimension reasoning is 3 sentences: data-driven case, what's holding it back, what would change the score. Replicate the structure.
3. **Bias mechanic.** Constructive framing appears in headline + first sentence of each pillar narrative; risks appear with mitigation, not omission. The counter-view is real, named, and answered with data — not dismissed.
4. **Plain English.** Acronyms expanded on first use (LGA, ERP, BTS, BTR, ICSEA, APRA, LVR). No trading-desk slang. Planning jargon translated. See [SKILL.md](SKILL.md) tone rule 6 for the full table.
5. **Land-constraint paragraph.** The supply-pipeline pillar walks DEMAND → SUPPLY → ABSORPTION → STATEWIDE → LAND → CONCLUSION. This paragraph structure is the spine of the buy case and should appear in every suburb where the data supports it.
6. **EXTENDED pillars.** This pilot did not populate all 12 EXTENDED pillars individually. Future runs should populate `comparable_sales` (done here), and where data is reachable also `macro_context`, `days_on_market`, `planning_zoning`, `schools`, `transport_amenity`, `climate_risk`, `crime_safety`, `tenure_mix`, `construction_cost`, `cashflow`, `tax`. Where data isn't reachable, emit the pillar with `"status": "data_not_available"` and a `reason`.
